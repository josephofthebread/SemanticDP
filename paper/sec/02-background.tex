\section{Background \& Literature Review}
Language models (LMs) are increasingly adapted using sensitive domain-specific data in social, biomedical, educational, and research settings. Differential privacy (DP) provides a rigorous framework for limiting individual-level privacy leakage through calibrated randomized mechanisms \citep{dwork2006calibrating,dwork2014algorithmic}. In LM adaptation, DP can reduce memorization and leakage, but it also affects optimization, generation behavior, and the semantic fidelity of learned or sanitized text. Much of the private learning literature evaluates this tradeoff primarily through downstream task accuracy or utility. For generative models, however, task utility alone is incomplete: a model may preserve aggregate benchmark performance while becoming less faithful to evidence, less calibrated, more prone to hallucination, or less reliable in following safety and instruction-alignment expectations.

We study privacy-preserving LM adaptation as a three-way tradeoff among privacy, utility, and alignment. Prior work on text sanitization and metric DP shows that sensitive information can be perturbed while partially preserving semantic structure \citep{chatzikokolakis2013broadening,carvalho2021tem,yue2021differential}. Related work on hallucination and semantic uncertainty argues that reliability failures in LLMs are often better understood at the level of meaning rather than surface token sequences \citep{farquhar2024detecting}. Semantic privacy research further suggests that privacy leakage can be contextual and inferential, not limited to verbatim memorization \citep{ma2025sok}. Together, these lines of work motivate a closer examination of how privacy mechanisms alter task-relevant meaning, and whether such semantic changes translate into factual or behavioral alignment failures.

Our work differs from prior privacy-utility evaluations by explicitly decomposing the effect of DP mechanisms into direct behavioral effects, semantic distortion, and downstream alignment degradation. Rather than assuming that stronger privacy guarantees monotonically degrade model quality, we ask whether measurable semantic distortion explains alignment failures beyond what is predicted by privacy budget alone. We evaluate several privacy mechanisms, including random token perturbation, semantic text sanitization, and DP-compatible fine-tuning, across truthfulness, hallucination, safety, refusal behavior, helpfulness, and instruction-following tasks, aiming to identify which forms of privacy-induced perturbation are most damaging to aligned generation, and which may be mitigated without abandoning strong privacy guarantees.
